# Abstract

Bayesian models explain perceptual estimation by proposing that observers combine uncertain sensory evidence with prior expectations. However, trial-level responses can cluster near either the sensory evidence or the prior, motivating a memoryless Switching Observer that selects a strategy independently on each trial. This account does not address whether inference modes persist over time.

We hypothesized that perceptual behaviour is governed by temporally persistent latent states, with observers remaining across trials in sensory-reliant, prior-reliant, or lapse modes. We reanalysed 83,210 motion-direction estimates from 12 participants across 388 independent subject-session-run sequences. Responses were modelled using von Mises emissions. We compared an independent switching mixture, three one-trial serial-dependence baselines, a static hidden Markov model (HMM), and a covariate HMM whose transition probabilities depended on sensory reliability, prior uncertainty, stimulus-prior conflict, and recent trial history. Models were fitted with multistart optimisation and evaluated using four-fold run-level cross-validation.

The covariate HMM achieved the highest predictive likelihood (-0.8133), followed by the static HMM (-0.8432), and independent switching (-0.9053). Relative to independent switching, the covariate and static HMMs improved held-out log-likelihood by 0.0919 and 0.0621 per trial, corresponding to predictive-density ratios of 1.096 and 1.064; bootstrap intervals excluded zero. The static HMM estimated high sensory and prior self-transition probabilities of 0.937 and 0.964, whereas one-back serial effects provided negligible improvement. Supplementary multinomial and survival analyses showed that prior uncertainty and sensory evidence predicted inferred state occupancy and persistence. With the prior state as reference, the interaction between prior uncertainty and coherence was significant for the sensory state (beta = 0.37, 95% CI [0.34, 0.40]) and the lapse state (beta = 0.07, 95% CI [0.03, 0.11]) in the covariate HMM.

These findings support a model in which observers remain in stable latent inference states across multiple trials before transitioning between them, rather than selecting strategies independently on each trial.
